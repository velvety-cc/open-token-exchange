This page is the project's reality check. Two **high-confidence** findings undercut the original
"arbitrage and speculate" pitch. Neither kills the project — but they redirect it.

## Finding 1 — There is no riskless arbitrage

The cross-provider price spread is real (>3×, see [Underlying](/research/underlying-asset)), but:

<Warning>
**A token is consumed at the moment of generation.** You cannot buy tokens cheap from one provider
and resell them. The "spread" is **cost-minimizing routing of your own consumption** — which the
aggregator already does automatically. There is no inventory to carry, no resale leg, no riskless
round-trip.
</Warning>

What people call "arbitrage" here is really one of:

- **Routing** — picking the cheapest endpoint for your own request (OpenRouter does this).
- **Basis trading** — betting the *gap* between two price series converges/diverges (real, but
  requires a tradeable instrument on *each* leg — which doesn't exist yet, and OTE would have to
  create it).

<Tip>
**Design consequence:** strike the word "arbitrage" from the product. The honest verbs are
**hedge**, **lock-in**, and **speculate on direction**.
</Tip>

## Finding 2 — Prices fall in one direction

Capability-normalized LLM output token prices fell **~40×** in roughly two years:

| When | Approx. output price (capability-normalized) |
|---|---|
| Early 2023 | ~$60 / M tokens |
| Early 2025 | < $1.50 / M tokens |

> "Token prices fell from approximately $60 per million ... to less than $1.5 ... a more than
> 40‑fold reduction." — arXiv:2603.21690, corroborated by [Epoch AI](https://epoch.ai/data-insights/llm-inference-price-trends)
> and a16z's "LLMflation."

<Warning>
This is a **secular decline**, not **bidirectional volatility**. A healthy two-sided speculative
market needs prices that surprise *both* ways. A market where everyone knows the trend is *down*
is structurally **short-biased**: longs have to be paid (via funding/discount) to take the other
side.
</Warning>

### Why a hedging product still works on a falling underlying

A one-directional underlying is **fine for hedging** even though it's weak for speculation:

- **Buyers of inference** fear the *rare up-move* (a provider hikes, or a new frontier model is
  expensive). They'll pay a premium for a **price cap / fixed-price forward**.
- **The downward trend funds the short side** — sellers of protection collect the secular decline as
  carry. This is a coherent, fundable market structure (similar to selling insurance on a risk that
  usually doesn't fire).

<Info>
**The unmeasured variable that decides everything: short-horizon volatility & reprice frequency.**
The 40× figure is a *long-run capability-normalized* trend. It says nothing about whether a *given*
model's price wiggles day-to-day. If OpenRouter reprices a specific model only every few months, an
*active trading* venue has nothing to do between repricings, and OTE collapses to a *forward/voucher*
product. **Measuring this is task #1** — see [Open Questions](/architecture/open-questions).
</Info>
