The strongest evidence that OTE is *feasible* comes not from token markets (which don't exist yet)
but from **compute markets that commodified in 2026**. They are structural analogues: a fragmented,
non-deliverable compute commodity made tradeable via a cash-settled index.

## 1. Regulated GPU compute futures (the precedent)

<CardGroup cols={2}>
<Card title="CME + Silicon Data" icon="building-columns">
Announced **2026‑05‑12**. First **compute futures**, USD-denominated, **cash-settled against the
Silicon Data benchmark index** rather than physically delivered. Covers H100/H200/B200/RTX5090
class.
</Card>
<Card title="ICE + Ornn" icon="building-columns">
Announced **2026‑05‑19**. USD-denominated, **cash-settled GPU compute futures** against the **OCPI**
index. A major regulated exchange treating compute as a listable commodity.
</Card>
</CardGroup>

> CME: "cash-settled against the benchmark index rather than physically delivered."
> ICE: "U.S. dollar denominated and cash-settled."

<Warning>
**Caveats (high time-sensitivity).** Both were announced ~1 month before this research and are
**pending regulatory review — not live trading**. They validate *feasibility and design*, not market
success. And the underlying is **GPU compute time, not LLM tokens** — a structural analogue, not a
direct precedent. Tokens are *administratively priced*; GPU rental is *market-cleared*.
</Warning>

## 2. The production daily index (the missing piece, already built)

The hardest part of OTE — a credible daily index over a fragmented market — **already exists for
compute** and the methodology transfers:

<Card title="Silicon Data — Silicon Index" icon="chart-line">
A **daily H100 rental index**, recalculated **every business day** from **~3.5M data points** across
providers/regions, distributed via **Bloomberg Terminal (ticker `SDH100RT`)** and a direct API.
Backed by DRW.
</Card>

<Info>
**Transferable pattern for OTE:** aggregate many points daily → publish one benchmark number → ship
it down an institutional feed path. Swap "GPU rental quotes across providers" for "token rate cards
across providers/models." This is the blueprint for the [OTE Price Index](/architecture/price-index).
Caveat: Silicon Data is a *single-vendor proprietary* index — OTE should aim for a *transparent,
reproducible* methodology to differentiate on trust.
</Info>

## 3. The academic design — Standard Inference Token (SIT)

<Card title="arXiv:2603.21690 — AI Token Futures Market" icon="graduation-cap">
Proposes a **Standard Inference Token (SIT)**: a token normalized to a fixed **capability bar** —
GPT‑4‑Turbo Jan‑2024 benchmarks (MMLU ≥ 86%, HumanEval ≥ 67%, GSM8K ≥ 92%). A Monte Carlo simulation
claims **62–78% volatility reduction** and a 12-month cost std-dev cut from **$1.80 → $0.65 / M SIT
(≈87%)**.
</Card>

<Warning>
**Confidence: medium.** Single-author, **non-peer-reviewed preprint**. The headline hedging numbers
are outputs of the author's **own simulation of a market that doesn't exist** — treat them as
illustrative, not empirical. The durable contribution is the **SIT idea**: anchoring "a token" to a
fixed quality bar so the price series isn't corrupted by models getting smarter. That's the single
most reusable artifact from the academic literature.
</Warning>

## Why SIT matters for OTE specifically

Without quality-normalization, your index conflates two things:

1. **Price falling because inference got cheaper** (what you want to track).
2. **Price falling because you're measuring a smarter/cheaper new model** (a units change).

<Tip>
SIT-style normalization lets OTE publish **two indices**: a raw "cheapest capable model" series
(what buyers actually pay) and a **capability-held-constant** series (a cleaner tradeable underlying).
The recommendation revisits this in [Price Index](/architecture/price-index).
</Tip>
