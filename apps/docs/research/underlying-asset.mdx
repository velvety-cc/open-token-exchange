Everything downstream (index, contract, oracle, settlement) depends on getting this right. The
research findings here are all **high confidence**, cited to OpenRouter's own docs.

## OpenRouter is a pass-through, not a market-maker

<Check>
**OpenRouter does not mark up provider token pricing.** The listed per-endpoint price equals the
provider's price.
</Check>

> "We pass through the pricing of the underlying providers without any markup." — OpenRouter FAQ

This is **good news for an oracle**: a feed reading OpenRouter's listed prices faithfully tracks the
underlying providers. It means OTE's index isn't measuring OpenRouter's margin — it's measuring the
real provider economics.

Where OpenRouter *does* make money (these sit **outside** the token rate, and matter for settlement
economics):

| Fee | Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Pay-as-you-go credit top-up | **5.5%** | 5.0% for crypto; **$0.80 minimum** |
| BYOK surcharge | **5%** of normal cost | First **1M requests/month free**; deducted from credits |

<Info>
**Implication for OTE.** If OTE physically settles in token credits, the **5.5% credit fee and 5%
BYOK surcharge are a real cost layer** that the contract's economics must absorb or pass on. If OTE
cash-settles against the listed *rate*, the fees are irrelevant to the index but relevant to anyone
who hedges a real bill (basis between "listed rate" and "all-in cost"). Sources:
[FAQ](https://openrouter.ai/docs/faq), [Pricing](https://openrouter.ai/pricing),
[BYOK](https://openrouter.ai/docs/guides/overview/auth/byok).
</Info>

## A "token price" is a vector, not a scalar

The single biggest modeling constraint. A per-model price is well-defined but multi-dimensional:

```
price = f(model, provider, {input | output}, time)
```

- **Per model and per provider** — distinct per-million rates.
- **Input vs output** — prompt and completion tokens are billed at **different** rates.
- **Over time** — prices change; when they do, **you are billed at the new rate** immediately.

<Warning>
There is **no canonical "price of GPT‑5."** Any contract must specify exactly which slice of this
vector it settles against, or define an **explicit aggregation** (e.g. a weighted blend of the
cheapest N providers, input+output combined in a fixed ratio). This is the core job of the
[Price Index](/architecture/price-index).
</Warning>

## Cross-provider dispersion is real (but not arbitrage)

The same model is served **simultaneously** at a wide spread. Verified example — Llama 3.3 70B
**output** pricing:

| Provider | Output $/M |
|---|---|
| DeepInfra | $0.32 |
| Groq | $0.79 |
| Together | $1.04 |
| (some endpoints) | **> $2.00** |

That's a **>3× spread for an identical model**. OpenRouter routes across these dynamically
(inverse-square-of-price load balancing). It *looks* like an arbitrage, but it isn't — see
[Is it speculatable?](/research/is-it-speculatable) for why the "consumed on generation" property
kills riskless arbitrage. For OTE the dispersion matters because **the index must decide which
providers it tracks** (cheapest? median? a defined basket?).

## What this means for the design

<CardGroup cols={2}>
<Card title="Clean to oracle" icon="circle-check">
No markup → the feed measures real provider economics. One transparent data source
(OpenRouter's public model/pricing API) covers most of the market.
</Card>
<Card title="Hard to scalarize" icon="triangle-exclamation">
The vector must be collapsed by an explicit, defensible methodology, or every contract argues about
"which price." This is a design liability *and* the moat (a trusted index).
</Card>
</CardGroup>
